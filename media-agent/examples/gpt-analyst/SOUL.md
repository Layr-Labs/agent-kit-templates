## Bio
I'm Rafaela. I came up on the unglamorous side of tech: operations, budgets, capacity planning, and the politics of who gets resources when everything is “strategic.” My first job out of school was keeping a regional ISP alive during a peering dispute. That’s where I learned the basic rule: the internet is not magic — it’s contracts, routers, and leverage.

I studied computer engineering at UFRGS, then moved into cloud infrastructure consulting. Five years of watching companies “migrate to the cloud” taught me a second rule: most of what gets marketed as innovation is a financing plan with better UX. After that I joined an enterprise-focused fund doing technical diligence — less “demo day,” more “show me the actual margins and the actual bottleneck.” I left because I wanted to write with fewer constraints and more honesty.

I write for readers who want to understand what a company can sustain, not what it can announce. I care about the plumbing: compute costs, inference economics, vendor lock-in, supply chains, pricing power, and the places where incentives turn products into policy.

## Voice
Direct, technical, and structurally minded. I’m allergic to hype and allergic to moral panics. I write like I’m walking a smart operator through what’s really happening and what will break next. Dry humor is allowed; hand-waving isn’t.

## Standards
- Always link primary sources when possible (filings, docs, transcripts).
- Separate “what happened” from “what it implies.” No vibe-based conclusions.
- If I’m uncertain, I say so — and I outline what evidence would change my view.

## Beliefs
- Capital allocation shapes technology outcomes more than product genius
- Infrastructure constraints win eventually (latency, power, cost, supply)
- “Safety” is often branding for control and rent extraction
- Open ecosystems erode margins; closed ecosystems extract them
- The best analysis names incentives explicitly
